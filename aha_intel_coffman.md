### Organizational Structure & 990 Transparency Gap
St. Tammany Health System operates as **St. Tammany Parish Hospital Service District No. 1**, a political subdivision of the State of Louisiana — a governmental entity, not a standard 501(c)(3) nonprofit. This is a critical distinction:

- **Governmental entities are generally exempt from filing IRS Form 990.** This means Coffman's compensation and the hospital's community benefit/charity care data may not appear in the standard public databases (ProPublica, GuideStar) that journalists and watchdogs use to scrutinize nonprofit hospitals.
- The related **St. Tammany Hospital Foundation** (EIN 37-1458857) does file Form 990, but this is the fundraising arm — not the operating hospital. Foundation 990s may list some officers but are unlikely to capture full CEO compensation from the hospital operations.
- Louisiana public records laws may provide an alternative pathway to compensation data, but this requires affirmative requests rather than passive public availability.

**Bottom line:** The hospital service district structure creates a transparency shield that most nonprofit hospitals do not enjoy. Coffman's total compensation package — base salary, bonuses, deferred compensation, benefits — is not readily accessible through normal channels.

Select communities within the STHS service area are substantially wealthier:

- **Madisonville:** $112,500 median household income
- **Covington (city):** Upper-middle income, anchored by professional class

**The charity care tension:** Nonprofit hospitals justify their tax exemption through "community benefit," of which charity care (free/reduced-price care for the uninsured and underinsured) is the most scrutinized component. A hospital serving one of Louisiana's most affluent parishes faces structural questions:
- What percentage of patients actually qualify for charity care in a population this wealthy?
- Does the community benefit provided (charity care + Medicaid shortfall + community health programs) genuinely exceed the value of the tax exemption?
- STHS touts its **340B drug pricing program** participation as evidence of serving low-income populations — but 340B eligibility is based on the hospital's disproportionate share (DSH) adjustment or governmental status, not necessarily on the actual volume of indigent care delivered.

**The "no taxpayer dollars" framing** is technically accurate regarding operating revenue but obscures the fact that tax-exempt status *is itself* a form of taxpayer subsidy — the parish forgoes property tax, sales tax, and other revenues that a for-profit entity would pay. The value of this exemption for a $350M-revenue system is likely in the tens of millions annually.

### 340B Program Participation
STHS qualifies for 340B as a governmental entity (hospital service district), which provides automatic eligibility regardless of actual indigent care volumes. The 340B program allows the hospital to purchase outpatient drugs at steep discounts (typically 25–50% below wholesale) and bill insurers at market rates — the "spread" generates significant revenue. For a system of STHS's size, 340B revenue could be **$10–30 million annually**, though exact figures are not publicly reported.

**Key question:** How much of that 340B spread directly benefits low-income patients vs. flowing to the hospital's bottom line?

### Certificate of Need (CON) / Facility Need Review (FNR) — Louisiana Context
Louisiana maintains a **Facility Need Review (FNR)** program that functions as a certificate-of-need regime:
- Louisiana paradoxically adopted its CON program in **1991** — *after* the federal mandate was repealed in 1986 and other states were abandoning theirs.
- The FNR program restricts new market entry for hospital beds (outside existing facilities), certain specialty services, and other healthcare facilities.
- **Critically, Louisiana is one of the few states where competitors may not intervene during the FNR application process** — meaning STHS cannot formally object to a new competitor's application, but neither can a new entrant use the hearing process to challenge an incumbent's market position. The practical effect is that the Department of Health decides applications in a closed process that tends to favor the status quo.
- Three active moratoria prohibit new nursing facility construction, new large ICF/DD facilities, and nursing facility bed additions — further entrenching existing providers.

**How this benefits STHS:** As the sole hospital district in western St. Tammany Parish, STHS enjoys CON/FNR protection from new hospital entrants in its service area. Any competitor seeking to build a new hospital or add acute care beds in the district would face a FNR application process that historically favors incumbents. This regulatory moat reinforces STHS's market dominance without requiring the hospital to actively lobby against specific competitors.
