## LOBBYING & POLITICAL

### AHA Litigation Under Golder's Legal Leadership

**Price Transparency (AHA v. Azar / AHA v. Becerra)**
- AHA filed suit (Dec 2019) challenging CMS's hospital price transparency final rule, arguing it exceeded statutory authority by requiring disclosure of privately negotiated insurer rates (not just "gross charges"). AHA also raised First Amendment commercial speech and chilling-effect arguments.
- **Lost at trial**: U.S. District Judge Carl Nichols ruled for HHS, finding CMS had authority.
- **Lost on appeal**: AHA appealed but the rule was upheld. The price transparency rule took effect Jan 1, 2021.
- Golder was Deputy GC during portions of this litigation and inherited the strategic posture as GC.

**Site-Neutral Payment Challenges**
- AHA has filed multiple suits challenging CMS site-neutral payment reductions (equalizing outpatient department and physician office reimbursement). Key filings include the "2020 Site Neutral Complaint," summary judgment briefs, and government appeals of AHA's initial wins.
- AHA initially won a challenge to site-neutral cuts but the government appealed.
- This remains an active litigation front; site-neutral policy is a top AHA priority to defeat.

**340B Drug Pricing — AHA v. Becerra (Supreme Court)**
- The Supreme Court heard *American Hospital Association v. Becerra* regarding HHS's authority to cut 340B reimbursement rates. AHA argued the cuts threatened access to care. The Supreme Court ruled **unanimously in AHA's favor** (2022), finding HHS had not conducted required surveys before cutting rates.
- Golder has described 340B as "a crucial lifeline for eligible hospitals."

**Post-Chevron Strategy**
- Following the Supreme Court's overturning of *Chevron* deference (2024, *Loper Bright*), Golder has publicly discussed AHA's strategic pivot: without Chevron, courts no longer must defer to agency interpretations, opening new litigation avenues against CMS rules AHA opposes.
- Golder has been vocal about using the post-Chevron landscape to challenge regulatory overreach.

**Anticompetitive Insurer Conduct**
- Under Golder, AHA has pursued regulatory relief to address what it characterizes as anticompetitive conduct by health insurers (prior authorization abuses, payment delays, market consolidation).

**Trump Administration Challenges (2025–)**
- AHA under Golder has filed or joined legal challenges to Trump administration actions affecting hospitals, though specific case names were not detailed in search results.

## PATTERN SUMMARY

**Chad Golder is a classic Washington legal operator** who followed the highest-prestige pipeline in American law: elite clerkships (Stevens, Garland) → DOJ career where he shaped major government litigation (ACA, LGBT rights, APA cases) → BigLaw partner at an elite firm's purpose-built DC influence office (Munger Tolles, alongside former SG Verrilli) → in-house General Counsel at the nation's most powerful hospital trade association.

**Key patterns:**

1. **Government-to-advocate revolving door**: Golder spent ~8+ years at DOJ working on healthcare regulatory enforcement, then pivoted to representing the regulated industry. His institutional knowledge of DOJ/HHS litigation strategy is his core asset to AHA. He is openly unapologetic about this dynamic.

2. **Litigation as lobbying**: Under Golder, AHA explicitly treats its legal department as an "advocacy practice." Litigation is not merely defensive — it is a strategic tool to delay, block, or overturn regulations AHA's hospital members oppose (price transparency, site-neutral payments, CMS reimbursement cuts). Win or lose in court, the litigation creates delay and shapes the regulatory negotiating environment.

3. **Post-Chevron opportunism**: Golder has signaled that AHA will aggressively exploit the end of Chevron deference to challenge CMS rules, repositioning decades of regulatory disputes as newly winnable without agency deference.

4. **Democratic legal establishment alignment**: His entire career network (Stevens, Garland, Verrilli, Obama DOJ) is center-left Democratic establishment. This does not prevent AHA from bipartisan lobbying — AHA's PAC gives to both parties — but Golder's personal network and policy instincts are rooted in the Obama-era legal infrastructure.

5. **High-value, low-visibility operator**: Unlike AHA's CEO or public lobbyists, Golder operates primarily through litigation strategy and legal advocacy rather than public-facing lobbying. His compensation and influence are substantial but less visible than AHA's registered lobbyists.

6. **Mixed litigation record**: The 340B Supreme Court win (unanimous) was a major victory. The price transparency losses were significant defeats. Site-neutral litigation remains contested. Golder inherited some of these cases but now owns the overall legal strategy.
