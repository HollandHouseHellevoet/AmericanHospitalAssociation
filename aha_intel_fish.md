- This is a physician-CEO actively lobbying to restrict the professional freedom of other physicians — on behalf of the hospital trade association. ## RED FLAGS

**1. Physician-Ownership Hypocrisy**
Fish is a physician (FACOG) who founded his own practice (Schneck OB/GYN, 2006) and rose through clinical leadership to the CEO suite. He now sits on the AHA Board — the primary lobbying body opposing physician ownership of hospitals. The AHA argues physician-owned hospitals create conflicts of interest and cherry-pick patients. Yet Fish's entire career trajectory demonstrates exactly the kind of physician entrepreneurship and clinical-to-executive pipeline that the AHA's anti-physician-ownership stance implicitly discourages. He is a physician who succeeded by acting like an owner — now helping to ensure other physicians cannot.

**2. Board Seat Concentration / Conflicts of Interest**
Fish simultaneously chairs SIHO Holdings (a hospital-affiliated insurance entity), sits on JC Bank's board, chairs Inspire Health Partners, serves on the Jackson County Industrial Development Corporation, and runs a $365M hospital system — all in a single small Indiana county. This creates overlapping fiduciary duties and potential self-dealing vectors:
- **SIHO Holdings:** A hospital-owned insurance company where Fish is Chairman. Schneck's relationship with SIHO as both owner and provider creates inherent conflicts in rate-setting and claims management.
- **JC Bank:** A hospital CEO sitting on a local bank board raises questions about lending relationships — does Schneck bank with JC Bank? Do Schneck employees have preferred banking relationships? Are there real estate or construction loans tied to hospital expansion?
- **Jackson County Industrial Development Corporation:** Fish influences both the county's economic development strategy and its largest employer/healthcare provider.

**3. Charity Care Opacity**
Schneck reports charity care as reductions to net patient service revenue — standard but opaque. For a county-owned hospital with tax-exempt status generating $365M+ in revenue, the community deserves transparency on:
- Actual dollar value of charity care provided vs. tax exemption value received
- Whether Schneck meets the AHA's own 15.1%-of-expenses benchmark for community benefit
- How charity care compares to executive compensation and capital spending

**4. County-Owned Structure Reduces Transparency**
Operating under Indiana Code 16-22 (County Hospital Law), Schneck's financial disclosures flow through state-filed audited financials rather than the standard 501(c)(3) Form 990 pathway. This means:
- Executive compensation may be fragmented across multiple entities (Schneck, HDC, Coordinated Health, SIHO)
- No single public document may capture Fish's total compensation package
- The county ownership structure provides a veneer of public accountability while creating disclosure gaps

**5. Small Hospital, Outsized AHA Influence**
Schneck is a single-site, ~1,200-employee community hospital in a county of ~44,000. Fish was appointed to fill a vacancy on the AHA Board, not elected through the normal process. The question is: what constituency does Fish actually represent on a national board, and whose interests does his appointment serve? Small independent hospital CEOs on AHA boards can provide useful rural/community perspective — or they can be easily managed votes for the larger system-dominated agenda.

**6. Noncompete Advocacy**
Fish's on-the-record testimony that physician noncompetes are "both necessary and useful" — delivered on behalf of the Indiana Hospital Association — positions him as an active advocate for restricting physician labor mobility. This is a policy area where hospital CEO interests directly conflict with physician interests, and Fish chose the hospital side.

Whether this concentration of influence serves the community or primarily serves the perpetuation of that influence is the fundamental question.
