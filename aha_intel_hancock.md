## FINANCIAL / COMPENSATION

### Sentara Health System Financials
- **System Revenue:** Sentara Health is a ~$10 billion integrated health system (combined hospital, physician, and health plan revenue). It ranks among the top 20 largest not-for-profit health systems nationally.
- **Net Assets:** Sentara has amassed approximately **$6 billion in net assets** — a figure repeatedly cited by critics noting the system pays no federal income tax as a 501(c)(3).
- **Tax-Exempt Entity:** Sentara Health (EIN: 52-1271901) files Form 990 with the IRS. The most recent filing was submitted November 6, 2025, covering the December 2024 tax year. Sentara Hospitals (EIN: 54-1547408) files separately.
- **Optima Health / Sentara Health Plans:** Sentara Health Plans (EIN: 54-1283337), operating as Optima Health, is a nonprofit health plan subsidiary with **over 1 million members** across commercial, Medicaid, and Medicare lines in Virginia and Florida. Sentara merged Virginia Premier into Optima Health in 2023, consolidating its insurance operations. Optima holds the **largest Medicaid market share in Virginia**, serving approximately 708,000 Medicaid recipients.

### Hancock Compensation
- Hancock became CFO on January 1, 2025, so her CFO-level compensation would first appear on Sentara's Form 990 for the December 2025 tax year (likely filed late 2026).
- **Prior disclosure:** As Chief Transformation Officer / EVP (2022–2024), Hancock would appear on Schedule J of Sentara's 990 filings if among the highest-compensated employees. Extracted data from recent filings is not yet available on ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer; the raw PDFs must be reviewed.
- **Compensation benchmarks:** Sentara's 2018 Form 990 disclosed 21 highest-compensated employees, with the top earner receiving **$6.1 million** (Michael M. Dudley, former officer, including related-organization compensation). The CFO of a $10 billion not-for-profit health system typically earns **$1.5–3.5 million** in total compensation (base salary + incentive + deferred compensation + benefits), based on comparable system CFO disclosures. Hancock's total package is likely in this range.
- **HFMA national chairmanship and AHA board service** are unpaid governance roles but confer significant reputational capital and network access that indirectly benefits career trajectory and compensation negotiating position.

### Charity Care vs. Tax Exemption Value
- Sentara's **$6 billion in net assets** have been described as "amassed partly because it pays no taxes" — with the system's finances effectively "underwritten by taxpayers."
- **Charity care adequacy questioned:** The Checks and Balances Project filed a formal IRS complaint (Form 13909) alleging Sentara's financial assistance policies are unclear, that it charges uninsured patients higher rates than insured patients, and that the value of its charity care may not justify its tax exemption.
- **DePaul Hospital closure:** Critics allege Sentara aggressively undermined DePaul Hospital — the primary charity care hospital in Hampton Roads — by poaching its physicians and opposing its Certificate of Public Need applications. DePaul, operated by Bon Secours, had the highest charity care rate in the region and closed in April 2021. The closure concentrated even more market power in Sentara.
- **The fundamental question:** Does Sentara's community benefit (charity care, community health programs, medical education) justify the hundreds of millions in foregone tax revenue from a system sitting on $6 billion in reserves? This is the same structural tension that exists across the nonprofit hospital sector, but Sentara's scale and market dominance in Virginia make it a particularly visible example.

### Vertical Integration Economics
- Sentara's ownership of both hospitals and Optima Health insurance creates a closed economic loop. A Virginia Joint Commission on Health Care (JCHC) analysis found that for a Level 3 Emergency Department visit, **Optima paid Sentara hospitals an average of $629**, while **other payers paid Sentara $1,159** for the same visit in the Virginia Beach-Norfolk-Newport News market.
- This pricing differential raises a critical question: is Sentara offering Optima members genuinely lower costs, or is it cross-subsidizing the insurance arm with artificially deflated transfer prices while charging external payers inflated rates? The JCHC found Sentara's integrated system "performs the same or worse on quality and access points" with costs "higher than competing non-integrated hospitals and insurers."
- **Competitor suffocation:** Chesapeake Regional Medical Center has publicly alleged that Optima deliberately restricts coverage of Chesapeake's services to funnel patients to Sentara facilities, creating an anticompetitive dynamic.

### Virginia Hospital & Healthcare Association (VHHA)
- Sentara is a major member institution of the **VHHA**, which serves as the state-level hospital trade association and coordinates with the AHA at the federal level.
- **HosPAC** is VHHA's political action committee. It raised over **$165,000 in 2021** to support candidates for statewide office and the Virginia General Assembly. Critically, eligible contributions raised through HosPAC are **shared with AHAPAC** (the AHA's federal PAC) — establishing a direct financial conduit from Virginia hospital money to AHA political activities.
- **VHHA federal lobbying:** VHHA has spent **$2.04 million across 78 lobbying disclosures** over the past decade. External firms include K&L Gates LLP ($450,000 since 2023) and Holland & Knight ($220,000 for Medicaid state-directed payment lobbying). Priorities include Medicare and Medicaid payment policies, rural hospital sustainability, 340B drug pricing program defense, and hospital workforce issues — all issues where AHA board members like Hancock set trade association policy.

### AHA Federal Lobbying Context
- The AHA is the single largest healthcare lobbying organization in the U.S., consistently spending **$25–30 million annually** on federal lobbying. As a board member, Hancock participates in governing the body that directs this spending.
- **AHA's core lobbying positions** directly relevant to Sentara's financial interests:
  - **Opposition to site-neutral payments:** AHA argues hospital outpatient departments have fundamentally different cost structures than physician offices. Site-neutral payment would reduce revenue for systems like Sentara that have acquired physician practices and billed at higher facility rates.
  - **Opposition to expanded price transparency mandates:** AHA has fought CMS rules requiring hospitals to post negotiated rates, arguing they are burdensome and potentially misleading. For a vertically integrated system like Sentara — where internal transfer pricing between Optima and Sentara hospitals is a key competitive lever — expanded transparency would expose the pricing differential that advantages its insurance arm.
  - **Defense of 340B drug pricing program:** Nonprofit hospitals like Sentara benefit enormously from 340B, which allows them to purchase drugs at steep discounts while potentially billing insurers at higher rates.
  - **Opposition to Medicaid payment reforms** that could reduce state-directed supplemental payments flowing to large hospital systems.

### The Structural Conflict
- Hancock's dual role as **Sentara CFO** and **AHA Board of Trustees member** creates a textbook structural conflict of interest. As CFO, her fiduciary duty is to maximize Sentara's financial performance. As an AHA trustee, she helps set the trade association's policy positions on the very federal regulations that determine Sentara's revenue.
- Every major AHA lobbying priority — opposing site-neutral payments, resisting price transparency, defending 340B, protecting Medicare reimbursement rates — directly increases revenue at Sentara Health.
- This is not unique to Hancock; the AHA board is *designed* to be populated by health system executives who have direct financial stakes in the policies the AHA advocates. But the CFO role makes it more explicit than usual: Hancock is the person who literally models the P&L impact of these policies at Sentara and then sits on the board that lobbies to shape them.

## RED FLAGS

### 1. Vertically Integrated Monopoly Governance
Sentara commands **over 70% of the patient market** in Hampton Roads, Virginia, and owns Optima Health, the largest Medicaid managed care plan in the state. The Virginia JCHC found that Sentara's integrated system charges external payers nearly **double** what Optima pays for the same services ($1,159 vs. $629 for a Level 3 ED visit), while performing "the same or worse on quality and access points" with costs "higher than competing non-integrated hospitals and insurers." Hancock, as CFO, is the architect of the financial strategy that maintains this pricing architecture. Her AHA board seat gives her influence over federal policy that could either entrench or challenge this model.

### 2. IRS Complaint and Tax-Exempt Status Questions
The Checks and Balances Project filed a formal **IRS Form 13909 complaint** against Sentara, alleging:
- Unclear and potentially deceptive financial assistance policies
- Charging uninsured patients higher rates than insured patients (the opposite of what charity care obligations imply)
- Using its "Transparent Pricing Tool" to display prices that were lower than what patients were actually billed
- Aggressive conduct against competitors (particularly DePaul Hospital) inconsistent with charitable purpose
- If the IRS determined the violations were egregious, Sentara could face loss of tax-exempt status and potential back-tax liability that could consume a significant portion of its $6 billion in reserves.

### 3. DePaul Hospital Closure and Competitor Elimination
DePaul Hospital, operated by Bon Secours, was the **primary charity care hospital in Hampton Roads** — the facility with the highest rate of charity care in the region. Sentara allegedly:
- Poached DePaul's physicians
- Fought Bon Secours DePaul's Certificate of Public Need (COPN) applications to update programs
- Systematically undermined DePaul's financial viability
DePaul closed in **April 2021** — the same year Hancock joined Sentara. The closure eliminated Sentara's most community-benefit-oriented competitor and concentrated market power further. Whether Hancock participated in strategic decisions related to DePaul's competitive position during her early tenure at Sentara is unknown but worth investigating.

### 4. CFO-to-Trade-Association-Board Pipeline
Hancock's career arc is a case study in the revolving door between health system finance, consulting, and trade association governance:
- Big Four auditor (Ernst & Young, Deloitte) → health system finance → consulting partner advising on payment model transformation → health system CFO → AHA board member
- Each step deepens the alignment between personal career incentives and the institutional interest in maintaining the current reimbursement regime. Her HFMA national chairmanship (2015–16) and AHA Regional Policy Board service (2021–23) were stepping stones to the national board — a well-worn path that ensures the AHA board is populated by executives with deep financial stakes in the policies it advocates.

### 5. Political Access Through Personnel
Sentara's hiring of **Anna James** (former top aide to Governor McAuliffe) to run its government relations operation, while James's twin sister **Megan Healy** served in Governor Northam's administration, creates an unusual political access channel. This dual-family connection across two consecutive Virginia administrations is the kind of relationship that warrants scrutiny, particularly given Sentara's COPN battles and regulatory interests.

### 6. Optima Health's Competitive Practices
Chesapeake Regional Medical Center has publicly accused Optima Health of deliberately restricting coverage of Chesapeake's services to **funnel patients to Sentara's own facilities**. If true, this represents a health system using its insurance arm as a weapon against competitors — a textbook anticompetitive use of vertical integration. As CFO, Hancock oversees the financial relationship between Sentara hospitals and Optima Health Plans.

### 7. Transparency Resistance at Scale
Sentara's scale ($10B+ system, $6B reserves, 1M+ insurance members) combined with AHA's opposition to price transparency creates a compounding opacity problem. Hancock's dual role means the person responsible for Sentara's internal transfer pricing — the rates Optima pays Sentara hospitals, which the JCHC found are roughly half what external payers are charged — also governs the trade association fighting to keep those rates hidden from public scrutiny.

## PATTERN SUMMARY

**Melinda S. Hancock represents the purest expression of the CFO-to-trade-association-board pipeline.** Her entire career has been spent optimizing the financial performance of nonprofit hospital systems — from Big Four auditing to consulting on payment model transformation to running the books at three Virginia health systems — and she now governs the trade association whose primary function is protecting hospital revenue from federal regulation.

**The Sentara-specific angle is what makes this profile distinctive.** Most AHA board members run large health systems with the usual nonprofit hospital tensions (tax exemption vs. community benefit, market consolidation vs. competition). Sentara adds a layer: it is **vertically integrated with its own insurance company**, Optima Health, creating a closed economic system where the same entity controls both the provider and payer sides of healthcare transactions. The Virginia JCHC documented the pricing consequences — Optima pays Sentara roughly half what external payers are charged — and competitors have alleged Optima is weaponized to deny coverage at rival hospitals.

**The structural conflict is maximally legible with a CFO.** When a CEO sits on the AHA board, the financial incentive is present but diffused across their broader leadership responsibilities. When the CFO sits on the board, the connection is direct: Hancock models the revenue impact of site-neutral payments, price transparency mandates, and 340B reforms at Sentara, and then participates in setting the AHA's lobbying position on those exact policies. She doesn't merely benefit from AHA advocacy — she can quantify the benefit to the dollar.

**Key leverage points for public accountability:**
1. **Sentara's internal transfer pricing** — the rates Optima pays Sentara hospitals vs. what external payers are charged — is the smoking gun of vertical integration's anticompetitive potential. Push for full disclosure.
2. **Charity care vs. tax exemption value** — Sentara sits on $6 billion in reserves while an IRS complaint alleges it charges uninsured patients more than insured patients and contributed to the closure of the region's primary charity hospital. Demand specific dollar-for-dollar comparison.
3. **Hancock's compensation** — Once her CFO-year compensation appears on Sentara's Form 990 (expected late 2026), compare it to the system's charity care spending as a percentage of revenue. This ratio is the simplest measure of whether the nonprofit model serves the community or the executives.
4. **AHA board votes on price transparency and site-neutral payment** — Track whether Hancock recuses herself from policy votes that directly affect Sentara's revenue, or whether she participates without acknowledgment of the conflict. The latter is the expected pattern.
5. **Virginia legislative action on vertical integration** — State bills seeking to regulate vertically integrated systems' insurance practices (requiring Optima to cover services at competitor hospitals) directly threaten Sentara's business model. Monitor whether AHA resources or messaging are deployed to support Sentara's position in Richmond.

**Bottom line:** Hancock is a finance specialist whose career has been spent inside the hospital revenue machine, now placed on the board of the organization whose sole purpose is ensuring that machine keeps running at maximum capacity. The Sentara vertical integration model — hospitals plus insurance, dominant market share, aggressive competitor tactics, questionable charity care, $6 billion in tax-exempt reserves — is the extreme case of everything critics say is wrong with the nonprofit hospital sector. Having its CFO on the AHA board is not an accident; it is the system working as designed.
