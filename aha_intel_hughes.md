## LOBBYING & POLITICAL

### Hill Relationships & Network
- **18 years of Senate relationships** spanning both parties. Hughes: "One of the things that's fortunate in being in this town as long as I have been, you have a lot of relationships and colleagues and friends over many, many years." [Source](https://thehill.com/business-a-lobbying/lobbyist-profiles/544419-new-top-hospitals-lobbyist-battles-covid-public-option/)
- Key former bosses who define her network: **Sen. Don Nickles (R-OK)** (business partner 16 years), **Sen. Connie Mack III (R-FL)**, **Sen. William Cohen (R-ME)**.
- AHA contributes to **both parties** — including Biden, Pelosi, Schumer, McCarthy, McConnell, and both party senatorial/congressional campaign committees. [Source](https://www.opensecrets.org/orgs/american-hospital-assn/summary?id=D000000116)
- AHA lobbying team consists of former Hill staff: "They have all worked largely on Capitol Hill or they were deeply, deeply immersed in the issues."
- Rick Pollack: Hughes "has strong bipartisan respect that spans all corners of health care — members of Congress and staff, key regulatory departments within the Executive branch, as well as the private sector."

### Site-Neutral Payments — KILL/DELAY (AHA's #1 priority)
- Led AHA's aggressive campaign against site-neutral payment proposals that would equalize Medicare reimbursement between hospital outpatient departments and physician offices.
- **Attacked the Hassan-Cassidy framework** (Sens. Maggie Hassan, D-NH, and Bill Cassidy, R-LA): called it "irresponsible to think that clawing back up to $140 billion of Medicare spending for seniors won't destabilize access to care." [Source](https://www.healthcarefinancenews.com/news/aha-decries-site-neutral-payments-lower-cost-more-transparency-act)
- **Opposed the Lower Costs, More Transparency Act (H.R. 5378)** which passed the House 320-71 and included site-neutral provisions for drug administration services.
- Ran **digital and television ad campaigns** warning "more hospitals could shut their doors" under site-neutrality — critics view these as misleading given many large systems' profitability.
- Hughes: AHA spent "the better part of the year educating members, Senate and House side as well as staff, on the real consequences of site-neutral proposals."
- At the **2025 AHA Annual Membership Meeting**, Hughes warned of the House Energy and Commerce Committee's **$880 billion in potential funding cuts** via budget reconciliation. [Source](https://www.aha.org/news/headline/2025-05-05-ahas-hughes-discusses-advocacy-priorities-political-landscape-capitol-hill)

### 340B Drug Pricing Program — PROTECT STATUS QUO
- Hughes identified protecting 340B as one of AHA's **four most important issues**. [Source](https://www.aha.org/news/chairpersons-file/2025-01-27-chair-file-leadership-dialogue-tackling-todays-health-care-challenges-aha-leaders-stacey)
- AHA argues the program helps hospitals "stretch limited federal resources and expand health services to patients and communities."
- Government data shows a **22% jump in wholesale purchase discounts from 2021 to 2022** under 340B, drawing scrutiny from drug manufacturers and Congress. [Source](https://rollcall.com/2023/06/08/once-cushioned-from-lawmaker-scrutiny-hospitals-see-a-shift/)

### Price Transparency — WEAKEN/LIMIT
- AHA asks Congress to "review and streamline existing transparency policies to reduce patient confusion and regulatory burden" and to **refrain from advancing additional legislation** that could "confuse or complicate providers' ability to provide meaningful price estimates."
- Opposed site-neutral provisions bundled into transparency bills.
- **Pushed Congress to override HHS's crackdown on third-party web trackers** used by hospitals — effectively seeking to weaken digital privacy/transparency enforcement. [Source](https://www.fiercehealthcare.com/providers/hospitals-push-congress-override-hhs-crackdown-third-party-web-trackers)

### Hospital Consolidation / Antitrust — RESIST SCRUTINY
- AHA has resisted FTC and congressional scrutiny of hospital mergers and acquisitions.
- At a **2021 Senate Antitrust Subcommittee hearing**, AHA Board Chair Rod Hochman was the **only witness (out of six) to tout the benefits of consolidation**, while senators from both parties and other experts focused on drawbacks. [Source](https://washingtonmonthly.com/2021/05/21/the-hospital-industrys-no-good-very-bad-day/)
- AHA has been "critical of the approach and framework the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has employed to review hospital transactions."
- Hughes dismissed arguments from Rep. Cathy McMorris Rodgers that paying hospitals more for outpatient services drives consolidation through acquisition of independent physician practices.

### Surprise Billing — WEAKENED FINAL LEGISLATION
- AHA was among a "handful of organizations that managed to temporarily derail legislation to eliminate surprise billing, before ultimately making sure that the reforms that did pass were weak." [Source](https://washingtonmonthly.com/2021/05/21/the-hospital-industrys-no-good-very-bad-day/)

### Public Health Insurance Option — KILLED (2021)
- When Hughes started in early 2021, she immediately mobilized against proposals for a **public health insurance option**, which the hospital industry viewed as an existential threat. Successfully prevented its inclusion in final legislation. [Source](https://thehill.com/business-a-lobbying/lobbyist-profiles/544419-new-top-hospitals-lobbyist-battles-covid-public-option/)

## PATTERN SUMMARY

**Stacey Hughes is a career Senate insider turned elite healthcare lobbyist who now runs one of Washington's most powerful advocacy operations.** Her pattern is consistent and well-documented:

1. **Senate-to-K-Street-to-Trade-Association Pipeline:** 18 years building bipartisan Senate relationships → monetized those relationships at The Nickles Group for 16 years → moved in-house to AHA to directly wield a $27M+ annual lobbying budget. Each step deepened her influence and reduced intermediaries.

2. **Kill or Weaken, Never Reform:** Across every major hospital accountability proposal — site-neutral payments, price transparency enforcement, 340B reform, antitrust scrutiny, surprise billing — Hughes's AHA has followed the same playbook: delay legislation, water down what passes, and frame any cost reduction as a threat to patient access. The public option was killed outright. Site-neutral payments remain blocked in the Senate. Surprise billing reforms were weakened before passage.

3. **Fear as Strategy:** AHA under Hughes deploys fear-based messaging (hospital closures, access loss) to defend the financial interests of an industry that includes many of the most profitable health systems in the country. This messaging is amplified through ad campaigns, grassroots mobilization of 5,000 member hospitals, and direct congressional lobbying across every office.

4. **Bipartisan Access, Partisan Utility:** Hughes's network spans both parties, and AHA contributes to both sides. This makes her effective regardless of which party controls Congress — she can always find allies to block, delay, or dilute reforms.

5. **Vulnerability:** The political environment is shifting. Bipartisan frustration with hospital pricing, consolidation, and 340B opacity is growing. Hughes's own public statements acknowledge the difficulty of the current landscape. Her effectiveness is being tested as former allies reconsider the hospital industry's privileged position.
