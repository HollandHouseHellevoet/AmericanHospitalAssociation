# AHA Intelligence Dossier: Philip O. Ozuah, MD, PhD

## Subject
**Philip O. Ozuah, MD, PhD**
President & CEO, Montefiore Medicine (a/k/a Montefiore Einstein)
Bronx, New York
AHA Board of Trustees Member (effective January 1, 2025 — filling one-year unexpired term)

---

## AFFILIATIONS

### Montefiore Medicine / Montefiore Einstein
- President & CEO of Montefiore Medicine, the umbrella organization for Montefiore Health System and Albert Einstein College of Medicine.
- Oversees 13 member hospitals, 250+ ambulatory care centers, 10,000 physicians, and 53,000 employees across four counties (Bronx, Westchester, Rockland, Orange).
- Previously served as President of Montefiore Health System (2018–2019) and Executive Vice President & Chief Operating Officer (2012–2018).
- Chairman of Pediatrics, Albert Einstein College of Medicine (prior role).

### Career Arc
- MD from University of Ibadan, Nigeria; Master's in Education from University of Southern California.
- Rose through academic medicine and hospital administration at Montefiore/Einstein before becoming the system's top executive.
- Recognized by *Modern Healthcare* as one of the top physician executives in the country.
- Inductee, Alpha Omega Alpha Honor Medical Society.

### AHA Involvement
- AHA Board of Trustees member effective January 1, 2025 (one-year unexpired term).
- Member, AHA Health Systems Committee, 2020–2024; chaired the committee in 2023.
- Member, AHA Task Force on Primary Care, 2021.
- Board seat connects him to AHA's lobbying apparatus, PAC governance, and federal policy agenda on Medicare reimbursement, price transparency, site-neutral payment, and 340B drug pricing.

### Other Board and Institutional Ties
- Board member at Montefiore-affiliated holding companies (Montefiore SS Holdings LLC, Montefiore MV Holdings LLC, Montefiore HA Holdings LLC) — entities established in 2013 to own the land and buildings leased to system hospitals.
- Ties to the New York City Police Foundation (contributor/board connection) — a point of controversy given Montefiore's safety-net mission.

### Market Dominance: Bronx and Westchester
- Montefiore is the dominant health system in the Bronx — the poorest congressional district in the United States, where 27.3% of residents (44% of children) live below the poverty line.
- 75–85% of Montefiore patients are covered by Medicaid or Medicare, accounting for approximately 68% of system revenue.
- Following the 2008 recession, Montefiore pursued an aggressive acquisition strategy, growing from 4 hospitals in 2010 to 11 by 2019, absorbing facilities across the lower Hudson Valley, White Plains, New Rochelle, and greater Westchester.
- Acquired 16 hospital facilities from 12 healthcare systems over roughly a decade.
- Montefiore serves as the essential safety-net provider for a population with few alternatives — creating a near-monopoly dynamic in the Bronx.

### The "Early Retirement" Maneuver (2018)
- Ozuah collected a $9M early retirement payout in 2018 while transitioning from President of Montefiore Health System to CEO of the newly structured Montefiore Medicine umbrella entity.
- He never actually retired. The payout appears to have been triggered by a technical role change within the same system — a common executive compensation mechanism that converts deferred compensation into immediate cash at a structural inflection point.
- This was first reported by the *New York Post*.

### Charity Care vs. Tax Exemption Value
- Montefiore claims a mission of providing "one standard of excellent care to all patients regardless of their backgrounds or ability to pay" since 1884.
- 75–85% of patients are on Medicaid/Medicare. The Bronx patient population is among the poorest in the nation.
- Montefiore benefits from federal income tax exemption, state and local property tax exemptions, tax-deductible donations, and access to tax-exempt bond financing.
- The system is heavily dependent on taxpayer funding: FEMA reimbursements, the 1115 Medicaid Waiver program, DSRIP (Delivery System Reform Incentive Payment) funding, and the 340B Drug Pricing Program.
- **340B concern:** Montefiore participates in the 340B program — designed to provide drug discounts to low-income and rural patients — but reporting suggests many hospitals pocket the spread rather than passing savings to patients. Whether Montefiore passes 340B savings to its overwhelmingly low-income patient base is a key unanswered question.
- **Community benefit reporting** is documented on IRS Form 990 Schedule H, available via ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer. The adequacy of Montefiore's charity care relative to the enormous tax subsidies it receives — while its CEO earns $16M+ and its Bronx facilities are described as overcrowded and understaffed — is a central tension.

### Merger & Acquisition Financial Profile
- Post-2008 acquisition spree: 4 hospitals → 11 (by 2019), plus 250 ambulatory centers, a nursing home, and a home care agency.
- Holding companies (established 2013) own real estate leased back to system hospitals — a structure that separates asset ownership from operating entities, potentially shielding real property from operating liabilities.
- Effective 12/31/2023, Montefiore HMO was sold to Elderserve Health Care, Inc.
- A $750M new hospital building is under construction in White Plains (completion expected 2028) — drawing sharp contrast with conditions at Bronx facilities where patients reportedly languish in hallways.

## LOBBYING & POLITICAL

### AHA-Level Political Activity
- As an AHA Board member, Ozuah participates in governance of one of the largest healthcare lobbying operations in the country.
- AHA political contributions are made under authority of the AHA Board of Trustees in consultation with the PAC Steering Committee, state hospital association partners, and hospital/health system leaders.
- AHA's lobbying priorities directly benefit Montefiore's financial model: opposing site-neutral payment reform (which would reduce facility fee revenue), defending 340B program eligibility, protecting Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement rates, and resisting price transparency mandates.
- AHA's full lobbying expenditure and PAC contribution data are tracked by OpenSecrets (opensecrets.org/orgs/american-hospital-assn/summary?id=D000000116).

### Personal Political Giving
- No significant personal federal political donation records for Philip Ozuah were identified in this research cycle. This does not rule out state-level giving, contributions through family members, or bundling activity.
- Further research recommended: FEC individual contributor search, New York State Board of Elections filings.

### NYPD Police Foundation Connection
- Ozuah has been linked to the New York City Police Foundation as a contributor or board-level participant.
- Critics (including the NYC Civic Audit) characterize this as a "disturbing reallocation of resources — from patient care to executive wealth, and from community health to police militarization" — particularly given that Montefiore's patient base is overwhelmingly low-income communities of color in the Bronx.
- This connection creates an optics problem: a nonprofit hospital CEO serving the poorest borough in NYC directing personal or institutional resources toward police expansion while nurses strike over staffing ratios.

### Institutional Lobbying Posture
- Montefiore, as a massive Medicaid/Medicare-dependent system, has direct financial interest in every major AHA lobbying priority: 340B defense, opposition to site-neutral payments, Medicaid DSH (disproportionate share hospital) funding, GME (graduate medical education) funding, and resistance to nonprofit hospital accountability legislation.
- Ozuah's AHA Board seat gives him a direct channel to shape federal policy positions that protect Montefiore's revenue model — while Montefiore simultaneously faces congressional scrutiny for how it uses its tax-exempt status.

## RED FLAGS

### 1. Congressional Investigation: Organ Transplant "Tourism" (2026)
- The House Ways and Means Committee (Reps. Jason Smith, R-Mo., and David Schweikert, R-Ariz.) sent letters directly to Dr. Ozuah demanding documents related to allegations that Montefiore Medical Center transplanted organs procured from American donors into wealthy foreign recipients who traveled to the U.S. specifically to receive transplants.
- Over 100,000 Americans remain on organ transplant waiting lists; thousands die waiting each year.
- Lawmakers explicitly questioned whether this practice violates Montefiore's community benefit obligations under its tax-exempt status.
- Subpoena threats were issued if Montefiore failed to comply by February 10, 2026.
- **Significance:** This is not a routine regulatory inquiry — it is a targeted congressional investigation with potential to challenge Montefiore's 501(c)(3) status.

### 2. Congressional Scrutiny: Nonprofit Hospital "Mission Creep" (2025)
- House Ways and Means oversight subcommittee hearing focused on tax-exempt hospitals' community spending, executive compensation, and "anticompetitive" behavior through provider consolidation.
- Montefiore was specifically cited as an example of the pattern under investigation.
- Calls were made for more stringent reporting requirements for nonprofit hospital community benefit spending.

### 3. The Two-Tier System: Bronx Disinvestment vs. Westchester Investment
- Nurses, medical residents, and union leaders have publicly accused Montefiore of operating a "two-tier health system" — investing heavily in suburban Westchester facilities (e.g., $750M White Plains hospital) while allowing Bronx facilities to deteriorate.
- White Plains patients get private rooms and capital investment; Bronx patients "languish in emergency rooms for days at a time" on hallway stretchers.
- Management proposed a "restructuring" plan for the Bronx that would reduce bed counts and transfer patients to suburban hospitals — effectively redirecting the safety-net population away from their community.

### 4. Nurses' Strike (2023)
- Nearly 15,000 NYSNA members from Montefiore, New York-Presbyterian, and Mount Sinai walked off the job.
- Montefiore nurses accused management of "corporate greed" and systematic "Bronx disinvestment."
- Core grievances: unsafe staffing ratios, overcrowded emergency departments, patients on hallway stretchers, and a management team earning tens of millions while frontline workers demanded basic resource adequacy.

### 5. Medical Resident Exploitation
- Montefiore medical residents publicly protested understaffing and long hours, describing themselves as "exploited."
- Residents reported routinely working 80-hour weeks, treating patients in hallways, and being among the lowest-paid medical residents in the Bronx.
- A first-year resident directly accused Montefiore leadership of "pursuing profits over patients."

### 6. Clinic Relocation Without Community Input
- Montefiore planned to relocate a Fordham Road clinic serving a largely immigrant and poor neighborhood 2.5 miles south — on the advice of McKinsey & Company — to save on real estate costs.
- No community input was sought. The New York State Academy of Family Physicians wrote directly to Dr. Ozuah urging reconsideration.
- The move would have disrupted care access for one of the most vulnerable patient populations in the system.

### 7. Janitorial Staff Class-Action Lawsuit
- Cleaning staff filed a class-action lawsuit alleging systematic denial of overtime pay, seeking to cover 200+ workers.
- Workers reported accumulating up to 80 hours per week without receiving legally mandated time-and-a-half overtime compensation.

### 8. OSHA Citation
- The U.S. Department of Labor / OSHA cited Montefiore Medical Center for workplace safety violations (January 2022).

### 9. The $9M "Retirement" That Wasn't
- Ozuah collected a $9M early retirement payout in 2018 while simultaneously transitioning to a higher role (CEO of the umbrella entity). He never retired. This has the hallmarks of a deferred compensation acceleration event disguised as a retirement benefit — a mechanism that triggered a large lump-sum payout without any actual separation from employment.

### 10. 340B Program Exploitation Risk
- Montefiore participates in the 340B Drug Pricing Program but there is no public evidence it passes drug discount savings to its overwhelmingly low-income patient population. If Montefiore is pocketing the 340B spread while its patients — 75–85% on Medicaid/Medicare — pay full price, this represents a direct betrayal of the program's intent.

## PATTERN SUMMARY

Philip Ozuah and Montefiore Medicine present one of the starkest cases in American healthcare of the contradiction between nonprofit mission and financial conduct.

**The Core Contradiction:**
Montefiore operates as the essential safety-net hospital system for the poorest congressional district in the United States. Its patients are overwhelmingly low-income (75–85% Medicaid/Medicare). Its Bronx facilities are described by its own nurses, residents, and community members as overcrowded, understaffed, and deteriorating. Meanwhile, its CEO earns $16M+ annually, its executive suite collectively takes home $32M+, and the system pours $750M into a gleaming new suburban hospital in White Plains while proposing to cut beds in the Bronx.

**The Pattern:**

1. **Extract from the poor, invest in the affluent.** Montefiore's acquisition strategy consolidated control over healthcare in the Bronx — where patients have no alternatives — then redirected capital investment toward wealthier Westchester communities. The Bronx subsidizes Westchester's growth. Nurses call it a "two-tier health system." The data supports them.

2. **Maximize executive compensation while pleading poverty on staffing.** Ozuah's $16.36M compensation in 2023 is 94x a Congressman's salary, earned while his nurses struck over unsafe staffing ratios and his residents protested 80-hour weeks treating patients in hallways. The system found $32M for nine executives but could not find the resources to staff its emergency departments adequately.

3. **Exploit every available public subsidy while resisting accountability.** Montefiore benefits from federal tax exemption, state/local property tax exemption, tax-exempt bond financing, FEMA reimbursements, 1115 Medicaid Waiver funding, DSRIP payments, and 340B drug pricing discounts. It simultaneously resists — through AHA lobbying — site-neutral payment reform, price transparency mandates, and stricter community benefit reporting requirements. The system takes maximum public subsidy while fighting minimum public accountability.

4. **Use structural complexity to obscure financial flows.** Compensation reported as $0 at subsidiary level but $16.36M through related organizations. Real estate held in separate holding companies. An "early retirement" payout of $9M triggered by a role change, not an actual retirement. The corporate structure makes it difficult for patients, regulators, and the public to follow the money.

5. **Leverage AHA Board position to protect the model.** Ozuah's seat on the AHA Board of Trustees is not merely honorific — it places him at the center of the hospital industry's lobbying apparatus at the exact moment Congress is investigating his institution. Every AHA policy position on 340B, site-neutral payments, nonprofit accountability, and Medicare reimbursement directly protects Montefiore's revenue model and Ozuah's compensation structure.

6. **Congressional exposure is escalating, not receding.** Montefiore is now subject to two active lines of congressional investigation — organ transplant practices (Ways and Means, 2026) and nonprofit hospital accountability (Ways and Means oversight subcommittee, 2025). The transplant investigation carries subpoena threats and directly challenges Montefiore's tax-exempt status. This is an institution under intensifying federal scrutiny, led by a CEO who sits on the AHA Board.

**Bottom Line:**
Ozuah represents a specific archetype on the AHA Board: the safety-net system CEO whose institution's nonprofit status is subsidized by the poverty of its patient population, whose personal compensation is extracted from that same population's suffering, and whose AHA Board position is used to lobby against the very accountability measures that would expose the gap between mission and conduct. Montefiore's story is the strongest possible argument for nonprofit hospital reform — and its CEO sits on the board of the organization most aggressively fighting that reform.
