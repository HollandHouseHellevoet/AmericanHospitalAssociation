### UnityPoint Health System Financials (Parent Entity: Iowa Health System, EIN 42-1435199)

### Charity Care vs. Tax Exemption Value — The Core Tension
- **The national pattern**: Studies consistently show that nonprofit hospitals' tax exemptions (property tax, income tax, sales tax, tax-exempt bond financing) often exceed the value of charity care and community benefit they provide. This is the central vulnerability of the nonprofit hospital model.
- **Iowa context**: Iowa exempts nonprofit hospitals from property tax, state income tax, and sales tax. For a system the size of UnityPoint, the aggregate tax exemption value across all locations likely runs into the **hundreds of millions of dollars annually**.
- **Fort Dodge specifically**: Trinity Regional Medical Center in Fort Dodge, as a community hospital in a low-income rural market, likely provides meaningful charity care relative to its size — but the question is whether it exceeds the tax exemption value. In rural Iowa markets with high Medicare/Medicaid payer mix, charity care numbers can be inflated by counting Medicaid shortfalls as "community benefit" under Schedule H reporting.
- **Schedule H reporting flexibility**: The IRS does not specify a minimum community benefit percentage. Hospitals self-report on Schedule H, and the categories are broad enough to include Medicaid shortfalls, health professions education, community health improvement, and "subsidized health services" — allowing systems to present community benefit figures that substantially exceed actual free care to uninsured patients.
- **UnityPoint's own framing**: UnityPoint emphasizes its Community Health Needs Assessments (conducted triennially per ACA requirements), telehealth expansion (2,499% increase since 2020), and its "Together We Care" program for free/reduced-cost services. These are standard ACA compliance activities that all 501(c)(3) hospitals must perform — not voluntary generosity.

## 3. LOBBYING & POLITICAL

### AHA's Lobbying Machine — Glasgo's Role Within It
- The AHA is one of the most powerful lobbying organizations in Washington, consistently spending $20M+ annually on federal lobbying. As a Board of Trustees member, Glasgo participates in governance of these lobbying priorities and budgets.
- Her specific value to AHA lobbying: she provides a rural hospital executive who can testify before Congress and meet with Iowa's congressional delegation (Senators Grassley and Ernst, plus Iowa's House members) with personal credibility on rural access issues. AHA routinely deploys board members for Hill visits tied to annual lobbying pushes.
- **AHA RPB 6** (Regional Policy Board 6) is where state-level policy positions are aggregated into AHA's national advocacy agenda. Glasgo's service on RPB 6 means she has directly shaped AHA's rural policy platform for the seven-state Upper Midwest region before ascending to the full Board.

### Iowa Hospital Association (IHA) — State-Level Advocacy
- **IHA PAC**: The Iowa Hospital Association operates a Political Action Committee that accepts contributions from full-time salaried hospital employees with managerial, professional, or supervisory responsibilities, hospital trustees, and employed physicians. As a president-level executive and IHA Board member, Glasgo is squarely in the donor-eligible class and leadership circle of IHA PAC.
- **IHA lobbying priorities** in Iowa include: Medicaid reimbursement rates, workforce shortage legislation, scope-of-practice battles, rural hospital funding, and opposition to any expansion of Certificate of Need requirements that could limit facility development.
- IHA is a registered lobbying client in the Iowa Legislature's lobbyist database. IHA's lobbying operation works in close coordination with AHA's national agenda.

### Certificate of Need (CON) in Iowa
- **Iowa does NOT have a Certificate of Need law.** Iowa is one of approximately 15 states that have repealed or never enacted CON requirements. This means hospitals and health systems in Iowa can build facilities, add beds, and acquire equipment without state regulatory approval.
- **Implication for UnityPoint/Fort Dodge**: The absence of CON allows UnityPoint to expand services at Trinity Regional Medical Center (Fort Dodge) without regulatory barriers — which Glasgo has leveraged to add cardiovascular, orthopedic, oncology, and women's/children's services. However, the no-CON environment also means competitors could theoretically enter the Fort Dodge market, though the small population base makes this unlikely.
- **AHA's national CON stance**: AHA generally supports CON laws as protective of existing hospital market positions. Iowa's lack of CON is an interesting tension — UnityPoint benefits from the deregulated environment locally while AHA advocates for CON protections nationally.

## 4. RED FLAGS

### Flag 1: Executive Perks at a Nonprofit System
The Form 990 disclosure of first-class/charter travel, companion travel, and tax gross-up payments for UnityPoint/Iowa Health System executives raises questions about whether compensation practices at the system level are consistent with the charitable mission. While market-rate compensation is legally permissible for nonprofits, these perks at a system that serves rural, economically disadvantaged communities create an optics problem — particularly when contrasted with charity care obligations.

### Flag 2: Charity Care vs. Tax Exemption Gap
The perennial question for any nonprofit hospital trustee: does the community benefit provided by UnityPoint (and specifically Fort Dodge's Trinity Regional Medical Center) justify the tax exemption? The GAO has flagged IRS oversight of this question as inadequate nationally. In a rural Iowa market where the commercial payer base is thin and Medicare/Medicaid dominate, the actual "charity" component of community benefit (free care to uninsured) may be modest — while the tax exemption (property, income, sales, bond financing) remains substantial.

### Flag 3: Failed Sanford Health Merger (2018-2019)
UnityPoint's attempted merger with Sanford Health would have created a dominant Upper Midwest health system. The deal collapsed over governance disputes, but the attempt signals that UnityPoint's leadership (including the institutional culture Glasgo operates within) prioritizes scale and consolidation. This is relevant because AHA simultaneously advocates for rural hospital independence while its member systems pursue consolidation strategies that reduce rural hospital autonomy.

### Flag 4: Dual-Market Leadership as Cost-Cutting Signal
Glasgo simultaneously leading Fort Dodge and Sioux City (interim) from 2021-2024 could be framed as efficient leadership — or as a consolidation cost-saving measure where two markets share one executive. In rural healthcare, this pattern often precedes further service consolidation, staffing reductions, or market integration that can reduce local community control.

### Flag 5: The Rural Narrative vs. System Reality
Glasgo's public positioning is as a rural hospital champion — but she operates within UnityPoint, a $4–5B system with urban anchors. The rural hospitals (Fort Dodge, Sioux City) function as referral feeders for the system's higher-margin urban facilities. Her AHA board seat legitimizes AHA's rural advocacy, but the actual beneficiary of policies like increased Medicare reimbursement and 340B protection is the consolidated system, not the independent rural hospitals AHA's rhetoric invokes.

### Flag 6: No CON Law Creates Unregulated Expansion
Iowa's lack of Certificate of Need law means UnityPoint can expand services in Fort Dodge without state oversight of whether the expansion is needed or duplicative. While this benefits access in an underserved area, it also allows the system to build market dominance without regulatory check — particularly concerning when the same system's trade association (AHA) advocates for CON laws in other states that would protect incumbent hospitals from competition.

## 5. PATTERN SUMMARY

### The Pattern: Rural Credibility Serving System-Level Interests

Leah Glasgo represents a recurring pattern on the AHA Board of Trustees: **the rural hospital executive whose personal story of community service provides political cover for a large health system's institutional interests**.

**The narrative AHA deploys**: A nurse who rose through the ranks to lead a community hospital in small-town Iowa. She knows the struggles of rural healthcare firsthand — workforce shortages, thin margins, aging populations, distance to specialty care. When she testifies before Congress or meets with Iowa's delegation, she speaks with authentic rural credibility.

**The structural reality**: Glasgo operates within UnityPoint Health, a multi-billion-dollar system that benefits enormously from the policies AHA lobbies for — Medicare payment increases, 340B drug pricing protections, telehealth reimbursement permanence, and preservation of nonprofit tax exemptions. The rural hospital narrative is the vehicle; the system-level financial benefit is the destination.

**Key tensions**:
1. **Rural independence vs. system consolidation**: AHA advocates for rural hospital viability, but its member systems (like UnityPoint) consolidate rural hospitals into hub-and-spoke networks that reduce local autonomy. Glasgo's career arc — from independent critical access hospital CEO to system market president — literally embodies this consolidation pipeline.

2. **Community benefit vs. tax exemption**: UnityPoint claims substantial community benefit, but the reporting framework (IRS Schedule H) is so flexible that Medicaid shortfalls, education costs, and general "community health improvement" can be counted — inflating the appearance of charitable activity while the actual free care to uninsured patients may be a fraction of the tax exemption's value.

3. **Executive compensation vs. charitable mission**: First-class travel, companion travel, and tax gross-ups for executives at a nonprofit system that serves economically distressed rural Iowa communities is the kind of detail that fuels congressional skepticism about nonprofit hospital tax exemptions.

4. **No-CON advocacy tension**: Iowa's deregulated market benefits UnityPoint locally, but AHA nationally supports CON laws that protect incumbent hospitals from competition — a policy inconsistency that Glasgo's dual state/national roles straddle.

**Bottom line**: Glasgo is a credible, well-credentialed healthcare executive with genuine rural experience. She is not a bad actor — she is a *useful* actor for AHA's lobbying apparatus. Her appointment to the Board is strategic: it places a sympathetic rural face on an organization whose primary function is protecting the financial interests of America's hospital industry. Every rural hospital closure story, every workforce shortage anecdote, every telehealth success story from Fort Dodge becomes ammunition for AHA's $20M+ annual lobbying budget — which ultimately serves systems far larger and more profitable than Trinity Regional Medical Center.
