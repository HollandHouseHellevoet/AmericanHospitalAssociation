## LOBBYING & POLITICAL

### Registered Lobbyist
- Barwis is a **registered lobbyist in Connecticut** — an unusual credential for a sitting hospital CEO and noteworthy for an AHA board member. His lobbying registration appears to be connected to his role advocating on behalf of Bristol Health and the broader hospital industry at the state legislature.

### Legislative Advocacy (Documented)
- **Hospital tax fight (2011):** Barwis publicly confronted Governor Dannel Malloy, telling him Bristol Hospital would lose ~$4M over two years under Malloy's proposed 5.5% provider tax. Malloy's budget would have eliminated $83M in state funding hospitals receive for treating the uninsured/underinsured. Barwis called the proposal "shameful," saying "it's not like hospitals are making money hand over fist."
- **Hospital tax lobbying (2017):** Bristol Hospital was part of a coordinated CHA lobbying effort against the Connecticut hospital tax structure. Barwis was named CHA's 2017 Grassroots Champion specifically for his advocacy on eliminating the hospital tax and raising Medicaid rates.
- **Certificate of Need (CON) reform (2014):** Barwis was involved in legislative discussions around expanding CON oversight of hospital ownership changes. He publicly stated he hoped any legislation wouldn't create an imbalance between for-profit and nonprofit hospitals — at a time when Bristol Hospital was itself in talks with Tenet Healthcare (a for-profit chain partnered with Yale New Haven Health System) about a potential acquisition.

### AHA Grassroots Champion Award (2017)
- This award explicitly recognizes **lobbying effectiveness** — it is given for "exceptional leadership in advocating for hospital priorities" at the federal and state levels. Barwis's receipt of this award confirms he is deeply embedded in the AHA's legislative advocacy apparatus.

## RED FLAGS

### 1. Compensation-to-Performance Mismatch
Barwis earned $750K–$828K annually while Bristol Health posted operating losses in most years, culminating in a $21.8M loss in FY 2023 and a going-concern audit opinion. The bonus structure (up to 35% of salary) during years of persistent losses raises questions about compensation committee rigor, even with the claimed independent process.

### 2. Going-Concern Warning + AHA Board Appointment Timing
The independent audit flagging "substantial doubt" about Bristol Health's ability to continue operating was issued for FY ending Sept. 2023. Barwis's AHA Board term began Jan. 1, 2024 — just months later. An AHA board member running a system under going-concern doubt is extraordinary and raises questions about AHA's vetting process.

### 3. Nursing Home Sale to For-Profit
Bristol Health proposed selling Ingraham Manor (128-bed skilled nursing facility) to for-profit entities for $10.5M. The CT Office of Health Strategy ruled no CON was required. This asset liquidation during a cash crisis fits a pattern of distressed nonprofits shedding assets — potentially reducing community care access while converting nonprofit assets to for-profit ownership.

### 4. Tenet Healthcare Acquisition Talks
Barwis reportedly pursued acquisition by Tenet Healthcare (a for-profit chain) in partnership with Yale New Haven Health System. While this didn't materialize due to legislative CON expansion efforts, the willingness to sell a community nonprofit hospital to a for-profit operator is notable context for someone now sitting on the AHA board — which publicly champions the nonprofit hospital model.

### 5. Registered Lobbyist on AHA Board
Barwis being a registered lobbyist in Connecticut while serving on the AHA Board of Trustees creates a layered advocacy position: he simultaneously lobbies the CT legislature on behalf of his hospital, influences AHA federal policy positions, and benefits from AHA's own $30M+ annual lobbying spend. This is not inherently improper but creates multiple channels for influence that are worth tracking.

### 6. "Small Hospital" Paradox on AHA Board
Bristol Health (~$150–174M revenue, 154 beds) sits on the AHA Board alongside systems like HCA ($60B+), CommonSpirit ($34B+), and Kaiser ($100B+). The question is whether Barwis's seat represents genuine small-hospital voice or whether his sustained association leadership (CHA, ACHE, AHA regional boards) makes him an "insider's insider" who was cultivated for the board through loyalty to the association apparatus rather than institutional significance. His Wharton/CPA background and lobbying credential suggest someone who is politically sophisticated beyond his institutional scale.

### 7. CPA License Duration Discrepancy
Some sources state Barwis held a Pennsylvania CPA license until 1999; others say until 2007. A minor factual discrepancy, but worth noting for due diligence completeness.

### 8. State Budget Director Public Clash
CT budget director Benjamin Barnes publicly questioned Bristol Hospital's plans to build a new medical office building while simultaneously claiming it needed state funding. The hospital called the accusation "unfounded" — but the episode reveals a pattern where Bristol Health simultaneously claims financial distress and pursues capital projects.

## PATTERN SUMMARY

**Kurt A. Barwis represents a specific archetype on the AHA Board: the small-system CEO who ascends through sustained association service rather than institutional scale.**

### The Pathway
Barwis spent 20 years running a 154-bed community hospital while systematically accumulating leadership positions in CHA (board 2007, CEO Forum chair 2010), ACHE (Regent 2016, Governor), and AHA (Regional Policy Board delegate 2019, Board of Trustees 2024). Each role fed the next. His Wharton education, CPA background, and registered lobbyist status mark him as more politically and financially sophisticated than the typical small-hospital CEO.

### The Value to AHA
AHA benefits from having small-hospital voices on the board for two reasons: (1) it legitimizes AHA's claim to represent "all hospitals, not just big systems," and (2) small-hospital CEOs who owe their board seat to association loyalty are reliable votes for AHA leadership priorities. Barwis's Grassroots Champion award confirms he is a dependable advocate for AHA's legislative agenda.

### The Vulnerability
Bristol Health's financial trajectory — years of operating losses, a going-concern audit, a cash crisis requiring community fundraising, layoffs, and asset sales — undermines the credibility of an AHA board member. If Bristol Health fails or is acquired, it would be embarrassing for AHA. Meanwhile, Barwis's ~$800K compensation during sustained losses is the kind of data point that fuels populist criticism of nonprofit hospital executive pay.

### The CON Angle
Connecticut is a CON state. Research suggests CON laws can insulate incumbent hospitals from competition and enable CEO rent extraction through higher compensation. Barwis — running the only acute-care hospital in Bristol, protected by CON barriers to entry, while earning $800K+ during operating losses — fits this theoretical framework precisely. His lobbying against CON reform that might have enabled competition (or for-profit entry) while simultaneously pursuing a sale to Tenet Healthcare reveals a pragmatic operator working both sides of the CON debate.
