## RED FLAGS

### 1. ACLU Federal Lawsuit — EMTALA Violations (Denial of Emergency Pregnancy Care)
The ACLU and ACLU of Michigan filed a federal lawsuit against Trinity Health Corporation alleging "repeated and systematic failure to provide women suffering pregnancy complications with appropriate emergency abortions as required by federal law" (EMTALA). At one Trinity hospital alone, at least five women suffering miscarriages were denied urgent care due to Catholic Ethical and Religious Directives (ERDs). Patients reportedly hemorrhaged, contracted life-threatening infections, and suffered severe pain for days. Trinity sought dismissal, arguing a prior similar ACLU claim had been dismissed, and maintained the ERDs are "entirely consistent with high-quality health care."

### 2. Catholic Doctrine Service Restrictions — Reproductive Health Care Denial at Scale
Trinity's 93 hospitals operate under the ERDs issued by the U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops, which prohibit:
- Elective contraception (pills, IUDs, patches, rings)
- Elective sterilization (tubal ligations, vasectomies)
- Elective abortion
- Many infertility treatments
- Some forms of miscarriage management

**Impact data:**
- Research shows Catholic hospital acquisitions reduce tubal ligation rates by 31% per bed
- Tubal ligation is the second most common contraception method among U.S. women; ~700,000 performed annually, half post-delivery
- In one Texas study, nearly 50% of women denied post-delivery tubal ligation had an unplanned pregnancy within 12 months
- One-third of women whose primary hospital is Catholic are unaware of its Catholic affiliation; only 24% of Catholic hospitals mention ERDs on their websites
- Women of color and Medicaid patients are disproportionately affected
- Case study: A Michigan patient (Kalaina Sullivan) sought sterilization surgery, was refused by Trinity, went to an independent hospital — which Trinity then acquired, eliminating the alternative

### 3. Charity Care "Fair Share Deficit" — $784 Million Gap
As detailed in the Financial section, the Lown Institute finds Trinity receives an estimated $784 million more in tax exemption value than it returns in community benefit. This is the third-largest deficit among U.S. health systems. Critics argue this undermines the fundamental justification for nonprofit hospital tax exemptions.

### 4. Aggressive M&A — Market Concentration and Service Elimination
- **MercyOne acquisition:** Trinity absorbed MercyOne's 16 medical centers and 400+ sites of care in Iowa, adding $3 billion in revenue — a "mega" deal that significantly expanded Trinity's Midwest footprint
- **Premier Health acquisition** and other transactions bring total known acquisitions to at least 6
- **Rural/regional market dominance concern:** When Trinity acquires a community's only or primary hospital, patients lose access to reproductive health services with no local alternative. The Sullivan case illustrates this directly — an independent hospital was absorbed, eliminating a reproductive care option
- **Divestiture:** Trinity Health System (3 hospitals in eastern Ohio) is being negotiated for sale to UPMC/CommonSpirit, suggesting selective portfolio management
- Academic research confirms that hospital consolidation leads to higher prices, and a dominant system can leverage market power in one region to negotiate higher prices in another
- 43% of 2025 healthcare M&A transactions involved financially distressed parties — acquisition targets may have limited bargaining power

### 5. Data Breach Litigation

### 6. COVID Overpayment Dispute

## PATTERN SUMMARY

**The Core Tension:** Michael Slubowski leads a $23.9 billion tax-exempt Catholic health system that simultaneously (a) claims $1.3 billion in annual community benefit to justify its tax exemptions, (b) carries a $784 million "fair share deficit" indicating it receives far more in tax breaks than it gives back, (c) pays its CEO $5.3 million annually, (d) restricts access to basic reproductive health services across 93 hospitals in 26 states, and (e) aggressively acquires community hospitals — eliminating local alternatives for patients who need the very services Trinity's religious directives prohibit.

**Pattern 1 — Grow, Restrict, Defend:** Trinity's M&A strategy expands its geographic footprint into rural and regional markets where it often becomes the dominant or sole provider. Upon acquisition, ERD-prohibited services (contraception, sterilization, some miscarriage care) are eliminated. The system then lobbies aggressively — $1.6M in federal lobbying, AHA and CHA board positions — to protect the 340B program and Medicaid reimbursement that sustain its market position, while resisting transparency and accountability measures around community benefit adequacy.

**Pattern 2 — Insider Ascension, Institutional Continuity:** Slubowski's career is almost entirely within Catholic health systems (Trinity → SCL Health → Trinity again). He was groomed within Trinity's own Health Networks division, sent to lead an affiliate system (SCL Health) as a proving ground, then recalled to lead the parent. This is not a reformer brought in from outside — it is institutional self-replication. Policy positions under his leadership reflect deep alignment with the Catholic health ministry's dual priorities: maintaining religious identity/authority and expanding market share.

**Pattern 3 — The 340B Dependency Loop:** Trinity publicly frames its $1 billion annual budget gap and 340B savings as essential to serving vulnerable populations. But the Lown Institute data shows the system's community benefit spending doesn't match its tax exemption value by $784 million. The 340B program effectively subsidizes system growth and executive compensation as much as it funds charity care. Slubowski's "death by a thousand cuts" framing positions Trinity as a victim of underfunding rather than a beneficiary of undertaxation.

**Pattern 4 — Regulatory Arbitrage via Religious Liberty:** The ERDs create a de facto two-tier health system: Catholic hospitals restrict services that secular hospitals provide as standard of care, yet both receive identical tax exemptions and government reimbursement. Trinity's legal defense — that ERDs are "entirely consistent with high-quality health care" — has been tested in federal court (ACLU v. Trinity Health) with allegations of EMTALA violations. The system benefits from religious liberty protections while operating at commercial scale.

**Strategic Relevance to AHA Board Service:** As an AHA Board member (effective January 2026), Slubowski brings Trinity's policy priorities — 340B preservation, Medicaid protection, opposition to site-neutral payments, and defense of religious health system prerogatives — directly into the hospital industry's unified lobbying apparatus. His presence on the board ensures that the interests of large Catholic systems (which represent 4 of the 5 largest nonprofit networks nationally) are embedded in AHA's advocacy positions. Any AHA policy stance on reproductive health access, community benefit standards, or merger review will be shaped by voices like Slubowski's.
