## LOBBYING & POLITICAL

### Key Legislation — Positions Directed Under Her Leadership

#### Site-Neutral Payments (OPPOSE)
- AHA's flagship defensive priority. Kidder Hrobsky's team has led opposition to every iteration of site-neutral payment legislation, which would equalize Medicare reimbursement between hospital outpatient departments (HOPDs) and independent physician offices. AHA's position: "There is nothing 'neutral' about site-neutral policies" — arguing they fail to recognize legitimate cost differences between settings.
- In 2023, AHA sent letters to Congress opposing the House Energy & Commerce bill containing site-neutral provisions for drug administration payments. The AHA and Federation of American Hospitals jointly lobbied against these provisions.
- In 2024, Sens. Bill Cassidy (R-LA) and Maggie Hassan (D-NH) proposed extending site-neutral policy to all off-campus HOPDs by removing the 2015 Bipartisan Budget Act's grandfathering exception — a direct threat to AHA's prior legislative wins.
- In 2025, AHA published a fact sheet arguing that site-neutral proposals under consideration "would jeopardize access to care for patients and communities," and launched a grassroots "Action Needed" campaign urging members to contact lawmakers.
- **Stakes:** Critics estimate site-neutral reform could save Medicare up to **$180 billion** — the scale of what AHA is defending against.

#### 340B Drug Pricing Program (PROTECT)
- AHA strongly supports the 340B program and opposes efforts to impose new reporting requirements on 340B hospitals or cut Medicare payments to 340B participants.
- In 2023, the House Energy & Commerce Committee advanced legislation that would add reporting requirements for 340B hospitals; AHA opposed and lobbied against it.
- In 2024, AHA submitted comments to MedPAC urging caution against recommending 340B payment cuts.
- AHA **filed a federal lawsuit** (U.S. District Court, District of Maine) to block HHS's 340B Rebate Model Pilot Program, which would have replaced upfront drug discounts with after-the-fact rebates. AHA secured a **preliminary injunction** halting the pilot, with the court finding likely violations of the Administrative Procedure Act. HHS subsequently agreed to abandon the pilot.

#### Physician-Owned Hospital Ban (PROTECT)
- AHA opposes any legislative effort to lift or ease the Affordable Care Act's restrictions on physician-owned hospitals, arguing that such facilities cherry-pick less complex, more profitable patients and provide limited or no emergency services.
- This has been a 15+ year AHA lobbying priority. Critics (including a notable Substack analysis) argue AHA's opposition is motivated by protecting incumbent hospital profit margins rather than patient safety.

#### Hospital at Home (SUPPORT WITH CONDITIONS)
- Kidder Hrobsky has written to Congress advocating for long-term stability of the hospital-at-home waiver program, which gained bipartisan traction. AHA supports the concept but seeks regulatory frameworks favorable to health systems.

#### Rural Hospital Support (SUPPORT)
- AHA has advocated for the Rural Hospital Support Act; Kidder Hrobsky authored letters outlining the legislation's potential impact on rural hospital finances.

#### Behavioral Health Regulation Reform (SUPPORT)
- AHA lobbied Congress in 2023 to reform "discriminatory and outdated" behavioral health payment policies. Kidder Hrobsky stated that "arbitrary and outdated payment policies continue to reflect the undervaluing of behavioral health services."

#### Healthcare Workforce (SUPPORT)
- A top-tier 2025 priority. AHA advocates for legislative action on healthcare workforce shortages, and Kidder Hrobsky has noted bipartisan congressional interest.

### Congressional Committee Relationships
- **Primary jurisdictional targets:** House Energy & Commerce Committee (Health Subcommittee), Senate Finance Committee, Senate HELP Committee, House Ways & Means Committee (Health Subcommittee). These four committees hold jurisdiction over Medicare payment policy, 340B, site-neutral rules, and hospital regulation.
- **Senate Special Committee on Aging:** Kidder Hrobsky's former employer — she maintains alumni relationships from her staff tenure there.
- AHAPAC distributes contributions to members of both parties sitting on these committees, creating access for AHA's lobbying operation. The PAC's bipartisan posture is strategic: hospital policy issues cross party lines, and AHA needs allies on both sides of the aisle for defensive positioning.

### Grassroots & Member Mobilization
- Kidder Hrobsky oversees AHA's grassroots advocacy infrastructure, which activates hospital CEOs and board members to contact their congressional delegations. This "grasstops" strategy (leveraging prominent local employers) is a force multiplier beyond traditional PAC contributions.

## PATTERN SUMMARY

**Lisa Kidder Hrobsky is the operational center of the American Hospital Association's federal influence machine.** Her career arc reveals a deliberate accumulation of leverage points:

1. **Hill-to-K-Street Pipeline:** Senate committee staff → insurance trade group → hospital trade group → state association CEO → back to the national trade group at SVP level. Each move expanded her network and institutional knowledge while maintaining continuous healthcare policy focus. The revolving door is not incidental — it is the architecture of her career.

2. **Triple-Threat Role:** She simultaneously controls (a) AHA's direct lobbying operation (~$29M/year), (b) AHAPAC's candidate contribution strategy (~$3.4M/cycle), and (c) grassroots/grasstops mobilization of ~5,000 hospital systems. This concentration of legislative, political, and mobilization authority in a single executive is unusual and makes her the de facto gatekeeper for hospital industry access to Congress.

3. **Defensive Posture Dominance:** The bulk of AHA's legislative agenda under her direction is defensive — blocking site-neutral payments, preventing 340B transparency requirements, maintaining the physician-owned hospital ban, and litigating against executive-branch reforms. The pattern is one of **incumbency protection**: preserving existing payment advantages, market protections, and regulatory carve-outs rather than advancing new policy.

4. **Committee Targeting:** Her relationship network is concentrated on the four committees with healthcare jurisdiction: House Energy & Commerce, House Ways & Means, Senate Finance, and Senate HELP. Her alumni status on the Senate Special Committee on Aging adds a fifth node. AHAPAC contributions are strategically deployed to members of these committees, creating a reinforcing loop of access → contribution → access.

5. **Bipartisan Shield:** AHA's deliberate bipartisanship (contributing to both parties, framing issues as "access to care" rather than industry interest) insulates the organization from partisan attack and ensures that no single election cycle can eliminate its influence. Kidder Hrobsky manages this positioning.

6. **Litigation as Backstop:** The successful 340B injunction demonstrates that when legislative and regulatory channels fail, AHA under Kidder Hrobsky's direction will escalate to federal court — adding a judicial dimension to the influence toolkit.

**Bottom Line:** Kidder Hrobsky is not merely a lobbyist; she is the strategic architect of a multi-channel influence operation that deploys $30M+ annually across lobbying, PAC contributions, grassroots activation, and litigation to protect the economic interests of America's hospital industry. Any entity seeking to advance site-neutral reform, 340B restructuring, physician-owned hospital deregulation, or price transparency enforcement should expect her operation to be the primary organized opposition.
