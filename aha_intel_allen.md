## FINANCIAL / COMPENSATION

### Intermountain Health System Financials (2023)
- **Total revenue:** ~$15.7 billion system-wide; IHC Health Services Inc. (primary operating subsidiary) reported **$9.6 billion** in revenue.
- **Operating income:** $137 million (**0.9% margin**), up from $121 million (0.9%) in 2022. Management has stated a target of **3%–6% sustainable margin**.
- **Employees:** 59,000+ caregivers system-wide; IHC Health Services employed 51,256 individuals compensated $4.3 billion (avg. ~$84K).
- **Tax status:** 501(c)(3) nonprofit. EIN 87-0269232 (parent); EIN 94-2854057 (IHC Health Services).

### CEO Compensation — Rob Allen
- **Exact figure not publicly confirmed in available sources.** Allen is listed as President/CEO on Schedule J of the IHC Health Services Form 990 (tax year 2023, filed November 2024). The Paddock Post reported on 2023 executive compensation but the specific article is paywalled.
- **Context benchmark:** The 30 most highly compensated Intermountain executives received **$62 million** in 2023. Former CFO Albert Zimmerli was the highest-paid at **$8.3 million** (2023) and **$33 million cumulative** (2016–2023). Allen's compensation as CEO would be reported on Schedule J of Form 990 and is likely in the **$3M–$6M range** based on peer system CEO benchmarks and the fact that Zimmerli's separation/deferred comp inflated the CFO figure.
- **Historical exec comp:** 30 executives received a cumulative **$250 million** from 2016–2023. 9,226 employees earned >$100K in 2023.

### Tax Exemption vs. Community Benefit
- **Estimated tax benefit of nonprofit status:** Intermountain paid **$469 million** in taxes in 2023 (payroll taxes, marketplace fees, some property taxes, unrelated business income taxes). Had it been for-profit, it would have owed an additional **$362 million** in taxes.
- **Reported community benefit:** Intermountain claims **$746 million** in direct community benefit plus **$567 million** in health services investment = **$1.3 billion total** in 2023, which it frames as more than double its tax exemption value.
- **Charity care ratio:** The $1.3B figure includes Medicaid shortfalls, education, research, and broad "community building" — the narrowly defined charity care (uncompensated care to patients) is a subset. Specific charity care dollar figures require Schedule H of Form 990.
- **Utah context:** Utah is among the **top 5 states** with the highest tax benefit per hospital bed for nonprofit hospitals (alongside Massachusetts, Alaska, Illinois, and Indiana).

### Risk-Based Contracting & Insurance
- In **Utah**, ~50% of Intermountain's care operates under **risk-based contracts**.
- In **Nevada**, nearly **95%** of care is risk-based.
- Intermountain operates **SelectHealth**, its own insurance plan covering ~1 million members in Utah and Idaho — creating a vertically integrated payer-provider model.

### 340B Drug Pricing Program
- Intermountain facilities participate in the **340B program**. Utah Valley Regional Medical Center (Provo, 395-bed Level II trauma center) is a documented 340B participant.
- Intermountain uses 340B savings to fund a **charity care voucher program** at its diabetes clinic — insulin at $5/month (Novolog) or $25/month (Lantus) vs. $500/month retail for qualifying patients.
- **Critical research note:** A 2020 study co-authored with researchers from the **Intermountain Healthcare Delivery Institute** found that 340B participation had **limited impact on charity care** at covered entities nationally — raising questions about whether the program's benefits flow to patients or to institutional margins.

### Market Dominance in Utah
- Intermountain is the **dominant health system in Utah** by hospital count, clinic footprint, and insurance enrollment. With 33 hospitals and 385+ clinics across 7 states, the Utah market is its core.
- Primary competitors in Utah: **University of Utah Health**, **HCA MountainStar** (for-profit), **Steward Health Care** (limited presence).
- Intermountain's vertical integration (hospitals + clinics + SelectHealth insurance + pharmacy) gives it significant leverage in contract negotiations with employers and other payers.

## RED FLAGS

### 1. Tax Exemption Vulnerability — Landmark Legal Precedent
- In **Utah County v. Intermountain Health Care, Inc. (1985)**, the **Utah Supreme Court** reversed the Tax Commission's property tax exemption for Utah Valley Hospital and American Fork Hospital, finding the exemption was **not constitutionally permissible**. The court noted that surplus funds from one hospital could flow to facilities in other counties or to administrative costs, and that IHC's policy was to charge patients "whenever possible."
- This case became a **national precedent** in nonprofit hospital tax-exemption law and remains a vulnerability in Intermountain's narrative. The system has since aggressively expanded its community benefit reporting to counteract this history.

### 2. Community Benefit Accounting — Potential Inflation
- Intermountain's claimed **$1.3 billion in community benefit** includes Medicaid shortfalls, health professions education, research, and broad "community building activities" — categories that critics argue inflate the true charity care figure. The narrowly defined charity care (free or discounted care to uninsured/underinsured patients) is likely a fraction of the $1.3B headline.
- **Key question:** What is the Schedule H (Form 990) breakout between charity care, Medicaid shortfall, and other community benefit categories? The $362M tax benefit vs. $1.3B community benefit framing is Intermountain's own calculation and has not been independently audited by a disinterested party.

### 3. 340B Program — Internal Research Contradiction
- Intermountain's own researchers (Intermountain Healthcare Delivery Institute) co-authored a study finding that **340B participation had limited impact on charity care** nationally. While Intermountain showcases its insulin voucher program as a 340B success, the system-wide data on how much 340B revenue flows to patient charity vs. institutional margin is not publicly disclosed.

### 4. SCL Health Merger — Market Concentration Concerns
- The April 2022 merger with **SCL Health** created a 33-hospital system and expanded Intermountain into Colorado, Montana, and Kansas. The **Colorado Attorney General** reviewed the merger and issued an opinion allowing it to proceed, but the merger occurred during a period of heightened federal scrutiny of hospital consolidation.
- Post-merger integration challenges are common in large health system mergers; any service reductions, facility closures, or price increases in newly acquired SCL Health markets could generate political and regulatory backlash.

### 5. Vertical Integration — Payer-Provider Conflict of Interest
- Intermountain operates both the **provider network** (hospitals, clinics) and the **insurance plan** (SelectHealth, ~1M members). This vertical integration creates potential conflicts of interest in coverage determinations, network adequacy, and pricing — the same structural concerns raised about Kaiser Permanente and other integrated systems.

### 6. Executive Compensation Opacity
- While Intermountain's 30 top executives earned **$62M in 2023** and **$250M cumulative (2016–2023)**, Allen's specific compensation is not readily accessible without pulling the actual Form 990 Schedule J. The system does not voluntarily disclose CEO pay in its public communications — standard practice but a vulnerability given nonprofit scrutiny.

### 7. Prior Roles — Limited Public Record
- Allen's pre-Intermountain career as CEO of hospitals in **Wyoming, New Jersey, and Massachusetts** is referenced in official bios but **specific facility names are not disclosed**. This gap makes it difficult to assess his track record at those institutions (financial performance, quality metrics, community relations, any controversies).

## PATTERN SUMMARY

### The Profile
Rob Allen is a **career healthcare operator** — not a policy wonk, academic, or financier. His 30+ year trajectory from rural Wyoming hospital CEO to the helm of the 11th-largest U.S. nonprofit system follows the classic path of an operator who rose by managing mergers, expanding footprint, and optimizing operations. His BYU operations management degree and Utah State MBA reinforce the operational orientation.

### Strategic Posture
Allen leads a system that is:
1. **Dominant in Utah** with significant market power through vertical integration (hospitals + insurance + clinics + pharmacy).
2. **Aggressively expanding** via merger (SCL Health) into surrounding mountain-west states.
3. **Defending nonprofit status** amid intensifying scrutiny — using a community benefit narrative ($1.3B) to justify $362M in tax exemptions.
4. **Participating in 340B** while its own researchers have published evidence that the program doesn't meaningfully increase charity care nationally.
5. **Politically modest** at the federal level ($230K lobbying) but strategically placed on AHA governance bodies that shape industry advocacy.

### AHA Board Value
Allen brings the AHA a voice from the **large integrated system** perspective — specifically the payer-provider model. His priorities (caregiver burnout, operational simplification, risk-based contracting) align with AHA's messaging on workforce and value-based care. His placement on the DSH Advisory Group and Health Systems Committee suggests AHA values his operational perspective on payment policy.

### Key Leverage Points for Engagement
- **Tax-exemption defense** — Allen is personally invested in the narrative that nonprofit hospitals deliver more community benefit than their tax exemption is worth. This is a core AHA lobbying position.
- **340B preservation** — Intermountain is a 340B participant and beneficiary; Allen would oppose restrictions on the program despite the internal research tension.
- **Anti-consolidation scrutiny** — Having just completed the SCL Health merger, Allen has a direct interest in preventing enhanced antitrust review of hospital mergers.
- **Rural health** — Allen's Wyoming roots and rural hospital experience make him a credible voice on rural health access, a bipartisan issue.
- **Workforce** — Allen has made caregiver burnout and workforce retention a signature issue since becoming CEO.

### Risk Assessment
**Medium risk.** No personal scandals or legal issues surfaced. The primary vulnerabilities are institutional: the 1985 Utah tax-exemption case, community benefit accounting questions, 340B contradictions, and vertical integration concerns. Allen's pre-Intermountain career opacity (unnamed hospitals in WY/NJ/MA) is a minor gap. His compensation, while likely substantial, is within expected range for a system of Intermountain's size. Political exposure is low given modest PAC activity and no personal contributions on record.
