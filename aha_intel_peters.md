### Certificate of Need (CON) — Critical Lobbying Target
- Michigan is one of approximately 35 states that maintain CON laws, which require state approval before hospitals can add beds, acquire major equipment, or open new facilities.
- **MHA's position: Fierce defender of CON.** Peters has publicly stated that MHA "stood tall to prevent any erosion in our Certificate of Need laws," calling CON essential to "ensuring access to quality, affordable care for all."
- **The structural reality:** CON laws function as a barrier to entry that protects incumbent hospitals — particularly dominant systems like Corewell Health — from new competition. By limiting new hospital construction and service expansion, CON preserves the market power of existing MHA members.
- **Who benefits:** Large integrated systems (Corewell, Beaumont/Spectrum predecessor entities, Henry Ford, Trinity Health) that already hold CON approvals benefit most. Smaller competitors, ambulatory surgery centers, and new market entrants are blocked.
- **Peters' dual conflict:** As MHA CEO defending CON, Peters protects his members' market power. As an AHA Board member, he carries this protectionist posture into national policy discussions where AHA shapes positions on competition, consolidation, and market entry.

### Medicaid & Federal Advocacy
- Peters has been vocal in opposing Medicaid funding cuts in the federal H.R. 1 Reconciliation Bill, warning that Michigan hospitals face **$6 billion+ in losses over 10 years**.
- He appeared on WDET (Detroit public radio) in March 2025 arguing that "protecting Medicaid protects Michigan."
- While Medicaid defense is broadly popular, it also protects hospital revenue streams — Michigan hospitals receive significant Medicaid reimbursement, and MHA's advocacy preserves this institutional funding.

## RED FLAGS

### 1. Structural Conflict: State Trade Association CEO on National Board
- Peters is the CEO of a state-level hospital trade association who now sits on the board of the national hospital trade association. This is not unusual within AHA's governance design — state association executives routinely cycle through AHA leadership — but it reveals **how the AHA board is structurally captured by insiders.** The people setting AHA's national policy agenda are the same people running state-level lobbying operations that benefit from that agenda. There is no independent check; the regulated industry governs both levels of the advocacy apparatus.

### 2. BCBSM Board Seat — Payer-Provider Dual Loyalty
- Peters simultaneously represents Michigan hospitals (which bill Blue Cross) and sits on the BCBSM Board of Directors and Executive Committee (which pays those bills). This is a textbook dual-loyalty conflict:
  - On the **MHA side**, his job is to maximize hospital reimbursement from insurers including BCBSM.
  - On the **BCBSM side**, his fiduciary duty is to the insurer and its members/policyholders.
  - On the **AHA side**, he shapes national positions on insurer-provider relations.
- BCBSM is Michigan's dominant insurer. Peters' presence on both sides of the negotiating table raises questions about whether rate negotiations are truly adversarial or whether they reflect a managed accommodation between entities that share board-level relationships.

### 3. CON Law Defense as Market Protection
- Peters' vigorous defense of Certificate of Need laws serves his members' financial interests by blocking competitive entry. While MHA frames CON as protecting "access and quality," health economists widely regard CON as an anti-competitive barrier that raises prices and protects incumbent hospitals. Peters carries this protectionist posture into AHA Board deliberations, where national policy on competition and consolidation is shaped.

### 4. Peters as Consolidation Apologist
- When confronted with evidence that hospital consolidation raises costs, Peters publicly disputed the characterization of hospitals as "profiteers" and argued that mergers are necessary for hospitals to bear fixed costs. He cited electronic medical records, liability insurance, and physician subsidization as justifications. This framing deflects from the documented price effects of consolidation and aligns with the interests of Michigan's largest systems — particularly Corewell Health — which benefit from reduced competition.

### 5. Triangulated Power: Peters–Freese Decker–AHA
- **Tina Freese Decker** (Corewell Health CEO) is 2025 AHA Chair. Peters sits on the AHA Board. Corewell is MHA's dominant member system. MHA's podcast and communications amplify Corewell's messaging. This creates a closed loop where:
  - The state association (MHA/Peters) advocates for policies that benefit the dominant system (Corewell/Freese Decker).
  - The dominant system's CEO chairs the national association (AHA).
  - The state association CEO sits on the national board.
  - All three entities share aligned interests in CON protection, consolidation defense, and Medicaid revenue preservation.

### 6. PAC Control + Fundraising Host Role
- Peters controlled HEALTHPAC disbursements (as Treasurer) and separately served as a fundraising host for state and congressional candidates. This dual role — institutional PAC gatekeeper and personal fundraising bundler — gives him unusual leverage over Michigan politicians who depend on hospital industry money. Legislators who might otherwise scrutinize hospital pricing, CON, or consolidation have a financial reason to defer to MHA's positions.

### 7. 34-Year Entrenchment
- Peters has spent his entire career at MHA — 34 years, rising from staff to CEO with an indefinite appointment. This is not a red flag per se, but it indicates deep institutional capture: Peters' professional identity, compensation, relationships, and worldview are entirely products of the hospital trade association ecosystem. There is no outside perspective, no career experience in patient advocacy, insurance, regulation, or direct care delivery. He is a career lobbyist who now sits on the board of the entity he lobbies for at the national level.

### 8. Governor's Office Access
- Peters was appointed by Gov. Whitmer to the Michigan Economic Recovery Council (COVID-era) and the 2020 Census Complete Count Committee. While these are advisory roles, they demonstrate a direct channel to the Governor's office that complements MHA's legislative lobbying. During COVID, hospital associations nationwide leveraged emergency advisory roles to shape funding distribution, regulatory waivers, and reopening policies in ways that benefited hospital systems.

### 9. Opacity of Service Corporation

Trade associations commonly use service corporations to generate revenue that subsidizes lobbying and to create commercial relationships with the same entities the association advocates for — a potential conflict of interest that warrants scrutiny.

## PATTERN SUMMARY

Brian Peters is the archetype of the AHA's self-reinforcing governance model: a career trade association executive who has spent 34 years inside a single lobbying organization, accumulated cross-sector board seats that create layered conflicts of interest, and now occupies a seat on the national board that sets policy for the industry he has spent his career protecting.

**The pattern has five interlocking elements:**

1. **Vertical integration of advocacy.** Peters runs state-level lobbying (MHA) and now participates in national-level policymaking (AHA Board), while also directing political money at both levels (HEALTHPAC Treasurer + AHAPAC Board). There is no separation between the entity that lobbies Michigan legislators, the entity that lobbies Congress, and the entity that sets the national hospital industry's policy positions. Peters sits at the nexus of all three.

2. **Payer-provider board capture.** His simultaneous service on the BCBSM Board and leadership of MHA collapses the adversarial tension between hospitals and insurers that is supposed to discipline pricing. When the trade association CEO for the hospitals sits on the board of the insurer that pays the hospitals, rate negotiations become an insider arrangement rather than a market mechanism.

3. **Anti-competitive policy defense as institutional mission.** MHA's fierce defense of Certificate of Need laws — led personally by Peters — functions as market protection for incumbent hospital systems. CON blocks new entrants, preserves monopoly and oligopoly market structures, and ensures that dominant systems like Corewell Health face minimal competitive pressure. Peters carries this anti-competitive posture into AHA Board deliberations where national positions on competition, consolidation, and market entry are formulated.

4. **Consolidation defense network.** Peters publicly defends hospital mergers. Corewell Health's CEO chairs the AHA. Peters sits on the AHA Board. MHA amplifies Corewell's messaging. This is not a coincidence — it is a coordinated alignment of interests where the state association, the dominant health system, and the national association mutually reinforce a narrative that consolidation is necessary, beneficial, and should not be challenged by regulators or policymakers.

5. **Political money as structural leverage.** Through HEALTHPAC (one of Michigan's largest PACs), personal fundraising hosting, and AHAPAC Board membership, Peters controls or influences significant flows of hospital industry money to politicians at both the state and federal level. This money creates a dependency relationship: legislators who receive hospital PAC contributions are less likely to challenge hospital pricing, CON protections, or consolidation. Peters is the human node connecting political money to policy outcomes.

**Bottom line:** Peters does not represent a deviation from the AHA's governance model — he *is* the model. A 34-year trade association insider with no outside career experience, layered board conflicts, PAC control, and direct governor-level access, now elevated to the national board where he will help set policy that directly benefits his state members, his dominant health system partner, and his own institutional position. The AHA Board is not a deliberative body that weighs competing interests; it is a club of insiders like Peters who share identical incentives and face no structural accountability to patients, employers, or the public.
